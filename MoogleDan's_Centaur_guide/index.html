Looking for a character that's straightforward to play, easy to win with, and a little more exotic than those minotaur fighters you've been throwing into the meat grinder for the last 20 games? Then centaurs are for you. Although they come with some seemingly brutal handicaps, their innate strengths are enormously useful, making them one of the easiest races to grab that first win with.

So, what do centaurs have to offer?

Species Mutations

The inherent handicaps centaurs have to deal with can be a little intimidating when you first look at them. They come with the Deformed mutation, reducing the AC value of any body armour they wear by half its base AC, and the combination of Herbivorous and Fast Metabolism makes the hunger game a little more stressful than normal. These make it difficult to play a centaur as a melee brute, as they can't rely so much on armour to keep them intact, and there's a high hunger cost each time you sit around healing.

Fortunately, their positive traits greatly outweigh these costs. Tough Skin gives them a free +3 to AC, compensating for the Deformed penalty for most light and medium armours, and gives them a little more survivability than the average robe-wearer in the early game. Hooves makes them significantly more capable in melee combat, and also replaces their access to boots (base AC 2, max enchantment +2) with access to centaur barding (base AC 4, max enchantment +4). Those two mutations combined overcome the Deformed penalty once you manage to pick up some decent equipment, and when mixed with their natural +10% HP, they can be played as serious bruisers in the late game.

Of course, the most important feature of centaurs is their excellent movement speed, which is enough to render defensive stats much less important (at least in the early- and mid-game). A centaur moves 30% faster than average, and as long as they have access to a ranged weapon and a safe pillar to prance around, they can kite any melee threat without fear. Get into the habit of tactical cowardice if you want to survive.

Skill Aptitudes

Although better off than trolls, centaurs have a fairly lousy set of skill aptitudes. Except for Fighting and Unarmed Combat (which get zeroes), all their melee skills are slightly below average (-1). Magical options are significantly worse, with -1s across the board except for Poison Magic (-2) and Spellcasting (a brutal -3). Defensive skills are the worst of all, with -3 in Armour, Shields, and Dodging (which is further penalized by their large size). Even their utility skill aptitudes suck; Traps gets a -2 (it's hard to spot hidden triggers from so high up), and the combination of Hooves, large size, and a -3 Stealth aptitude makes sneaky centaurs almost unheard of.

The only things centaurs really excel at are Invocations (+1) and ranged combat. With +1s in Slings and Crossbows and +3s in Throwing and Bows, many centaur players seek out solid ranged weapons as soon as they can. Skilled centaur archers can slaughter melee opponents without taking a hit, and most dangerous unique casters can't hit you with anything if you just keep them at the edge of your line of sight.

The Centaur Archer Build

By far the most popular option for playing a centaur is to get a bow as early as possible, at which point you can start dealing damage to enemies anywhere in your line of sight. Mixed with your speed, you can kite most melee threats with ease; suddenly, early hill giants and trolls are something to look forward to. Just make sure to watch your ammo...

Stat Points

Above all else, you need high dexterity, which is something centaurs don't begin with much of. Ignore strength and intelligence; you'll have decent strength to begin with and will naturally gain more as you level up, and intelligence is useless since you are in no way a spellcaster. Once your intelligence is safely at 8 or higher, dump every spare stat point you get into dexterity.

Equipment

Obviously, you're going to want a bow. You can start off with one by playing a Hunter or Arcane Marksman, but any background can find one early on, either as randomly generated equipment or, less painlessly, in the possession of an enemy centaur. Make sure to grab all the arrows you can find; early on, running out of ammunition is a serious threat.

Although you can fire arrows in melee without penalty, you'll want a melee weapon in order to conserve your ammo, and since you're using a bow, shields won't really be an option. Therefore, you'll eventually want a solid two-handed weapon to maximize damage when you actually go into melee with opponents, but early on the skill requirements for those will be much too high. Try to find a nice electrocution or draining branded light weapon early on, and eventually upgrade to heavier and heavier options as you improve your weapon skill.

Unarmed Combat is also an option, and one that trains slightly faster than weapons, but you miss out on the opportunity for branded or artifact weapons. The choice is yours.

You may eventually want to cast a few spells, but for most of the game you'll be functioning as a purely physical character. Since spell failure doesn't matter and your EV is already penalized by your size, you'll want the biggest, heaviest armour you can wear (unless you're fighting with Unarmed Combat, in which case anything heavier than scale mail will seriously reduce your attack speed). Slap on a barding as soon as you find one, and put on some plate armour once you have 18 strength. It may reduce your accuracy a little, but it's worth it for the added survivability. If you find a nicely enchanted lighter armour, or one that grants fire, cold, or poison resistance, definitely wear that instead for the early game. Your end goal should probably be a fully enchanted fire dragon armour, ice dragon armour, or (if you have your resistances covered elsewhere) pearl dragon armour. Save your scrolls of enchant armour for those.

While bows are weak and benefit highly from damage enchantments, try to conserve your scrolls of enchant weapon for the longbow you'll eventually be using. Preferably, that'll be a vorpal or venom longbow for maximum damage out of your normal arrows; while flaming and frost bows are also useful in the early game, you eventually get access to enough elemental ammo that they aren't necessary. Keep a stack of 100 regular arrows and 30 of each kind of specialty arrow and you'll be able to deal with any sort of resistance or vulnerability you come across without weighing yourself down too much. Dispersal branded arrows aren't quite so crucial; although you'll want to keep maybe 5 around in case of emergencies, you rarely need more than one to escape a particular crisis.

Although jewelry shows up too randomly for much definite advice, try to use those slots to plug any holes in your resistances. This should include magic resistance; centaurs have little natural magic resistance, so bringing a ring of protection from magic or two with you can help save you from an untimely paralysis or trip to the Abyss. That being said, since you'll spend most of the game kiting enemies from beyond their range of attack, you can focus on offense a little more safely; wearing a ring of slaying is rarely a bad idea.

Gods

Okawaru or Trog are the safest bets here by far. Both provide access to near-infinite ammo once you get their piety flowing, and both give you new weapons, hopefully granting you a beefy longbow early on. The main difference lies in the fact that Okawaru offers you useful armour gifts as well, while Trog has the better panic buttons.

You can easily get a 3-rune win with either of these choices, but you'll absolutely want to switch to the Shining One once you reach the extended game. A holy wrath longbow or two-handed weapon can wreak havoc against anything you'll find in the Hells, the Tomb, and Pandemonium, and it's very hard to beat the rN+++, healing on kills, angelic support, and area-of-effect burst attack TSO offers. On top of that, the ability to quickly switch to Zin for the mutation resistance and/or curing is always nice. Just make sure to learn Blink and get it online fast after leaving Trog or Okawaru to better deal with the penance.

Skills

Once you have a bow, focus Bows until it hits 20. If this is on turn 1, feel free to turn everything else off; you can survive Dungeon:1-2 safely stabbing weaker opponents with whatever melee weapons you find lying around and shooting the gnolls, adders, and humanoids wielding runed weapons to death from safety. Hopefully by then you've found a decent weapon of the type you'd like to use. Long blades and axes are recommended, though maces & flails works well too. The reaching advantage polearms offer is redundant when coupled with your bow, and their 2-handed weapons are inferior, so avoid those. Staves are also an option, but not a terribly competitive one. Once you have your chosen melee weapon, start training that as well.

Once you've gotten your weapon speed to its minimum delay, switch to training Fighting; the added HP is useful. Once you find a suit of armour heavier than ring mail, turn on Armour until it hits level 2, but don't sink too much experience into it while your combat skills need work. These skills will make up the majority of what you use throughout the game.

  • You'll eventually want your Bows skill to hit 27 for the added damage output you get. Try to hit 15-20 by the time you finish the Orcish Mines.
  • Fighting is another one worth maxing out, though this will take much longer than Bows; getting it to 10 by the time you hit the Lair is a good idea.
  • Your melee weapon skill should be leveled high enough to reach its minimum delay. This will take longer, but is worthwhile. Once you've mastered Bows and Fighting, feel free to raise it higher.
    • If using Unarmed Combat, make this a much higher priority. While you shouldn't neglect Fighting, UC is the more important choice. Try to keep them at a 2:1 ratio.
  • Armour is a pain to train, but worth it when you're wearing heavy body armour. Getting it to 5 by the end of Lair, 10 by D:20, and 15 by the end of the game is reasonable (but more is always welcome).
  • Dodge is another agonizing skill to train, but with your (eventually) impressive dexterity score, it pays off fairly quickly. By the time you find your dragon armour, you should be able to focus Dodge for a while in order to receive some very impressive evasion gains. Training it until your EV hits 20+ is a good idea, but don't bother with it until you've cleared Lair at the earliest.
  • Neglecting Traps for too long is always painful. Once you hit Lair, turn it on until it hits at least 6. Getting it to 10-12 by the end game is strongly recommended.
  • Invocations will matter early on if you worship Okawaru; train it until Finesse has about a 10% failure rate. Once you switch to TSO, focus Invocations until it hits 27.
  • Evocations can be ignored; you don't need wands when you have hundreds of rounds of multielemental damage output. Consider it only if you find an amazing rod early on.
  • Once you've got your dragon armour, you should consider learning some basic utility magic. Even with your low intelligence, there are a number of useful low level spells that can be acquired without too much trouble. Blink, Apportation, and Shroud of Golubria are all useful spells that you can master just by studying Translocations. Add in some skill in Charms and Air Magic and you can unlock Fly and Repel Missiles. Portal Projectile will take a little longer to train, but the ability to make your longbow a smite-targeted attack is tempting. If you're feeling really ambitious, you can hammer down on Charms until Haste becomes available, but it's better to duplicate the effect with a wand of hasting, especially since you won't be training Spellcasting very fast at all.

The Centaur Poker Build

One issue I always had with centaur archers was the fact that, in the late game, I found it much safer to fight things in melee range than at a distance. Monsters with torment are much less likely to do so when they have the option of simply punching you for a little bit of damage, and the Shining One's Cleansing Flame ability is amazingly effective when you have 27 Invocations and a horde of demonic or undead opponents around you. Therefore, this build ignores all ranged weapons and avoids wasting experience on skills you eventually stop using. It requires a bit more care in play, but results in a much hardier late game character.

Any background that provides you with a polearm could conceivably take this path. The reliance on the polearm's reaching brand diminishes as you develop a more experienced and heavily defended character, so other weapon choices or backgrounds are also valid, but surviving the early game with no ability to kite is a much less pleasant experience.

Melee Kiting

Although most pure melee fighters sacrifice the ability to kite enemies easily, as a centaur you are superhumanly fast. With a good polearm in hand, you can fight opponents who are safely out of reach almost as well as you could with a bow. Simply retreat from the enemy until you create an empty space, then use your reaching capacity to stab the guy (press 'v' to evoke your weapon, or simply Tab if nothing else is around). It forces you to pay much more attention to your fights than if you simply used a bow, but since the best polearms are one-handed weapons (tridents and demon tridents), you are able to also use a shield, greatly increasing your durability. Also, the lack of concern over ammo frees up your inventory and god choice significantly, and allows for a more varied gameplay style. Finally, any end-game quality melee weapon you wind up using will do more damage per hit than an arrow would, and that's before taking into account the power of your hooves.

By far the biggest disadvantage of melee kiting is that you can no longer kite dangerous casters at the edge of your line of sight. When faced with these opponents, consider fleeing until you've got the right resistances or sheer damage output to take him out without too much risk.

Stat Points

Just like before, dexterity is your best bet, even if strength may seem more important now. Even if you wind up wearing a large shield, high EV is generally more useful than high SH.

Equipment

The most obvious difference here is that you'll want to find a polearm as quickly as possible instead of a bow. Spears are common, tridents somewhat less so (expect to find one in the hands of an orc not too far down). Gnolls may show up with halberds early on; while these do much more damage, they're inaccurate and very slow to attack with for low-level characters. Only use one if it's got a very nice brand or huge enchantment bonus.

Also, keep an eye out for a blowgun and some poison and curare needles. You don't need to have any training in Throwing to use those two needle types well, and curare is amazing for softening up those dangerous uniques you can no longer pepper with arrows in safety. Wands and especially rods are also worth paying a bit more attention to; there'll be far more use for them now.

You'll want to start wearing a shield at some point, though it's less critical in the early game where most enemies only use melee. Try not to use a shield too far beyond your skill level; while centaurs are large and thus better able to use large shields, doing so at 0 Shields skill will hamper your attacks significantly.

Gods

Okawaru and Trog are still respectable, reliable choices, but you now have much less dependence on their ammo gifts. Makhleb's healing on kills is extremely useful at any point in the game, and the abilities he offers give you some decent ranged attacks and summoning capacity. The Invocations skill necessary to use him well will come in handy when you switch to TSO, so that's not wasted either. Yredelemnul is also a decent choice; stabbing opponents from behind a wall of shambling corpses is a perfectly good way to avoid injury. Zin's abilities are excellent for dealing with tough enemies and a centaur's hunger issues, mutation resistance is always welcome, and switching to TSO with near max piety speeds things up significantly. For something a little spicier, consider Nemelex Xobeh. He'll cover your need for ranged attacks, summoning, and a whole lot more, though his penance is a bit nasty if you still choose to switch to TSO in the end.

Cheibriados would be almost impossible to use well, what with your fast movement speed, and Fedhas Madash's powers rely on the food source you get the most benefit from. As for the magic-related gods (Sif Muna, Vehumet, and Kikubaaqudgha), you've got no use for most of the gifts they offer. And Xom... well, he's as much of an option as he ever is.

Skills

Same as before, except that there's no Bow training and you should focus your Polearms skill until you've gotten it to its minimum delay; this is very important if you want to be able to melee kite effectively. Keep training it at a normal pace for the rest of the game; you'll probably want to max it out eventually, but balance it with Fighting. The addition of the Shields skill is also a significant change. Because of its -3 aptitude, you probably won't want to level it up beyond what you need for the shield you are currently using (3 for bucklers, 9 for shields, 15 for large shields). Leveling beyond that is experience that could be better spent on offensive skills or more Dodging.

You may also want to put some training into the Evocations skill. Wands and rods are tremendously useful for giving you ranged attacks, and if you follow Nemelex you'll be training the skill anyway. The rod of Warding, rod of Destruction (ice), rod of Venom, rod of Inaccuracy, and lightning rod all provide you with useful spells.
